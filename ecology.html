Ecology

Plants produce a vast array of compounds that play no role in primary catabolic or biosynthetic pathways.

 

Secondary Metabolites function to:

1. Deter herbivores

2. Protect against pathogens

3. Influence the growth of surrounding plants

4. Facilitate symbiotic associations

5. Interact with pollinators

6. Protect against ultraviolet radiation or high temperatures

 

 

Allopathy refers to the chemical inhibition of one species by another.

 

The "inhibitory" chemical is released into the environment where it affects the development and growth of neighboring plants. Allelopathic chemicals can be present in any part of the plant.

 

Allopathic compounds may have originally evolved as compounds that deter pathogens or herbivores and later became a part of communication and interaction between higher plants.

Plants cope with rapidly changing leaf temperatures through the production of low molecular mass hydrocarbons: Isoprene and monoterpenes.

 

Isoprene is the single most abundant biogenic, non-methane hydrocarbon entering the atmosphere due to emission from plants in both temperate and tropical ecosystems.

 

Isoprene emission protects against heat flecks and reactive oxygen species by increasing membrane order, equivalent to a 10 °C  decrease in temperature.

 

 

“Terpenes of different sizes and composition are found throughout all classes of organisms”

 

“Larger terpenoid molecules are essential components of human metabolism and include: adrenal hormones, the membrane component cholesterol, lipid soluble vitamins A, E, and K”

 

“While not necessary for viability, terpenes enable plants to prosper in their specific environment… they may be flavors, scents, or act as growth regulators, defense secretions, or pollinator attractants.”

 

 

The Olfactory System

 

Terrestrial insects (and terrestrial mammals) have odorant binding proteins that help the volatile odorant molecules move into the aqueous environment surrounding the olfactory receptors.

 

Humans have an estimated 107 olfactory receptor neurons, and dogs up to 4 × 109. In contrast to this large number of olfactory neurons, a mammalian auditory nerve contains the axons of only 3 × 104 neurons.

 

Olfactory receptor cells undergo continuous turnover throughout adult life, and they were the first mammalian neurons discovered to do so. The receptor cells differentiate from basal cells in the epithelium, develop dendrites and axons that connect with the second-order cells in the olfactory bulb, and have life spans of about 60 days before they degenerate.

 

 

Intranasal delivery provides a practical, non-invasive method of bypassing the blood-brain barrier (BBB) to deliver therapeutic agents to the brain and spinal cord.

 

This is possible because of the unique connections that the olfactory and trigeminal nerves provide between the brain and external environment.

 

Both the olfactory and trigeminal nerves innervate the nasal cavity, providing a direct connection with the CNS

 

Extracellular delivery, rather than axonal transport, is strongly indicated by the short time frame (≤ 10 minutes) observed for IN therapeutics to reach the brain from the nasal mucosa.

 

Possible mechanisms of transport may involve bulk flow and diffusion within perineuronal channels, perivascular spaces, or lymphatic channels directly connected to brain tissue or cerebrospinal fluid [9].

 

 

 

 

Forest Therapy

 

Recently, Forest Therapy has emerged as a method to address stress states, and much data on the physiological and psychological relaxing effects of forest environments have been accumulated.

 

Previous studies have reported that viewing forest scenery, walking in forests, and exposure to forest aerosols can: increase parasympathetic nervous activity, relaxing the body and suppressing sympathetic nervous activity which is increased in stress states;  decrease cerebral blood flow in the prefrontal cortex, calming frantic thought patterns; and decreases salivary cortisol concentration of stress hormone.

 

In addition, visiting a forest enhanced natural killer-cell activity and improved immune function and the effect lasted 30 days. In subjective evaluations, it was reported that people feel more “comfortable,” “soothed,” and “natural” when experiencing a forest environment and that the “tension-anxiety,” “depression,” “anger-hostility,” “fatigue,” “confusion,” and “vigor” of the mood state profile improved.
